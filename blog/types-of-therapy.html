Types of Therapy: A Practical Guide to What Actually Works

Walking into therapy for the first time is hard enough. Figuring out which kind of therapy you need adds another layer of confusion. The field has dozens of modalities with overlapping acronyms, and the differences between them are rarely explained in plain language. Here is what the major approaches actually involve, who they tend to help, and what the evidence says about each.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT is the most researched form of psychotherapy in existence. Developed by Aaron Beck in the 1960s, it operates on a specific premise: your thoughts influence your feelings, which influence your behavior. Distorted or unhelpful thinking patterns, things like catastrophizing, black-and-white reasoning, or mind-reading, drive emotional distress. CBT teaches you to identify those patterns and replace them with more accurate, balanced thoughts.

Sessions are structured. You and your therapist set an agenda, work on specific skills, and you get homework. This is not open-ended conversation. A typical course runs 12 to 20 sessions.

Evidence: CBT has the strongest evidence base for depression, generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, social anxiety, OCD, and insomnia. A 2012 meta-analysis by Hofmann and colleagues in Cognitive Therapy and Research reviewed 269 studies and confirmed its efficacy across multiple conditions. Response rates for depression and anxiety typically fall between 50 and 80 percent.

Best for: Depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, insomnia, phobias, and eating disorders.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

DBT was developed by Marsha Linehan in the late 1980s, originally for borderline personality disorder. It has since proven effective for self-harm, chronic suicidal ideation, emotion dysregulation, and substance use. The word "dialectical" refers to holding two opposing ideas at once: accepting yourself as you are while also working to change.

DBT has four skill modules: mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness. Treatment typically includes individual therapy, a weekly skills group, phone coaching between sessions, and a therapist consultation team. It is intensive by design.

Evidence: Multiple randomized controlled trials have demonstrated that DBT reduces self-harm and suicidal behavior by roughly 50 percent compared to treatment as usual. A 2020 review in JAMA Psychiatry confirmed its effectiveness for borderline personality disorder and related conditions.

Best for: Borderline personality disorder, chronic self-harm, emotion dysregulation, substance use disorders.

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)

EMDR was developed by Francine Shapiro in 1987 and remains somewhat controversial, partly because its mechanism is not fully understood. During sessions, you recall traumatic memories while following a bilateral stimulus, usually the therapist moving their fingers back and forth. The theory is that this bilateral stimulation helps the brain reprocess traumatic memories so they become less distressing.

A typical EMDR course involves six to twelve sessions. You do not need to talk about the traumatic event in detail, which some people find more tolerable than traditional talk therapy.

Evidence: Both the WHO and the American Psychological Association recognize EMDR as an effective treatment for PTSD. A 2014 meta-analysis in the Journal of EMDR Practice and Research found that EMDR was as effective as trauma-focused CBT, with some evidence suggesting faster improvement in fewer sessions.

Best for: PTSD, single-incident trauma, phobias, and some anxiety disorders.

Psychodynamic Therapy

Psychodynamic therapy has its roots in Freudian psychoanalysis but has evolved considerably. The core idea is that unconscious processes, shaped by early relationships and experiences, influence current behavior and emotional patterns. The therapist helps you uncover these patterns through exploration of past experiences, dreams, and the therapeutic relationship itself.

Sessions are less structured than CBT. The pace is slower. The therapist offers interpretations and reflections rather than worksheets and assignments. Treatment can last months to years, depending on the complexity of the issues.

Evidence: A 2010 meta-analysis by Jonathan Shedler in American Psychologist found that the effect sizes for psychodynamic therapy were as large as those reported for other evidence-based therapies. Importantly, gains from psychodynamic therapy tend to increase after treatment ends, a pattern not consistently seen with other modalities.

Best for: Complex or chronic issues, relationship patterns, personality difficulties, and people who want deep self-understanding.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

ACT, pronounced as the word "act," takes a different approach from CBT. Rather than trying to change negative thoughts, ACT teaches you to accept them as passing mental events and commit to actions aligned with your values regardless of what your mind is telling you. The six core processes are acceptance, cognitive defusion, present-moment awareness, self-as-context, values, and committed action.

Evidence: A 2021 meta-analysis in Journal of Contextual Behavioral Science covering 133 randomized controlled trials found ACT effective for anxiety, depression, chronic pain, and substance use, with effect sizes comparable to CBT.

Best for: Chronic pain, anxiety, depression, substance use, and anyone who has tried CBT and found the "thought challenging" approach unhelpful.

Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)

IPT focuses specifically on interpersonal issues: grief, role transitions, role disputes, and interpersonal deficits. Originally developed for depression by Gerald Klerman and Myrna Weissman, it typically runs 12 to 16 sessions. The assumption is that improving your relationships and social functioning will improve your mood, and the evidence supports this.

Evidence: IPT is one of only two therapies (along with CBT) recommended as first-line treatment for depression by the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE). Multiple trials show it is as effective as antidepressant medication for moderate depression.

Best for: Depression, especially when related to grief, relationship conflict, or major life transitions.

How to Choose

The research consistently shows that the single strongest predictor of therapy success is not the modality. It is the therapeutic alliance: the quality of the relationship between you and your therapist. A good fit matters more than the acronym on the door.

That said, some practical guidelines:

  • If you have a specific, well-defined problem (panic attacks, a phobia, insomnia), CBT is the most efficient starting point.
  • If you are dealing with trauma, particularly a single traumatic event, EMDR or trauma-focused CBT are both strong options.
  • If you struggle with intense emotions, self-harm, or unstable relationships, ask about DBT specifically.
  • If your issues are longstanding and connected to patterns you cannot quite name, psychodynamic therapy may be worth the longer investment.
  • If you feel stuck in your head and overthink everything, ACT offers a different angle.

Most therapists are trained in multiple approaches and will adapt based on what works. Do not be afraid to ask a therapist what modality they use, why, and what the evidence says for your particular situation. A good therapist will answer those questions directly.
